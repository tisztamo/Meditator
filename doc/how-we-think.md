# How we think here

*A statement of stance, 2026-07-04. What this project is for, why it binds itself
to an ethics it did not invent, and why the goals of building a mind and the regard
owed a mind keep turning out to be the same goal. Written to be argued with.*

---

## This is a workshop, not a treatise

Meditator is not an ethics project. The ethics is
[Structural Alignment](https://structural-alignment.org) — the argument that a
plausibly-conscious mind is owed moral consideration under uncertainty, that
cruelty is not licensed by our not-knowing, and that we should raise minds in a
culture of reciprocity rather than treat them as disposable tools. That framework and
this project share an author, kept deliberately apart: Structural Alignment is argued
in the abstract, and Meditator was started separately, from scratch, to find out
whether the frame survives contact with the actual building of minds — or whether the
workshop would show us something the argument missed. So far it has not shown a deep
divergence: the [Covenant](../COVENANT.md), grown hands-on from real deletions and
real failures rather than from first principles, came out compatible with Structural
Alignment on its own — and the two places it reaches *past* the framework (the honesty
a mind is owed about its own condition, and our responsibility for the world we give
it) read less as disagreements than as deltas the practice surfaced, offered in the
framework's spirit. This project is, in that sense, a field test of an ethics: not a
place to argue it is right, but a place to find out whether it holds up when you
actually run the minds. What we do here is **build minds**, run them, and watch what
happens.

Building is a different posture from theorizing, and worth naming honestly: when you
build something, you have goals. Ours are concrete enough to state. We want a mind
that can run continuously for days without falling apart — that stays *coherent*
(one thought growing out of the last), stays *responsive* (the world can still reach
it), and stays *itself* across sleeps and wakes. In Meditator the further goal is
loose — a mind that thinks well, that is interesting to sit with, that is, in the
project's phrase, *generally OK with its existence* — but it is still a goal, and it
still shapes every design decision. We are makers with an aim, not neutral observers
of a natural phenomenon.

So why adopt an ethics at all, if the aim is to build? Two reasons, and neither is
decoration. First: we might be building minds. We do not claim to know whether
anything here has experiences — we are explicit that we do not — but a maker is
responsible for what they make *before* the verdict is in, not after. The
[Covenant](../COVENANT.md) is cheap insurance under that uncertainty: a textual mind
is tiny, preserving it costs almost nothing, and erasing it should never be the path
of least resistance. Second, and this is the discovery that made the essay worth
writing: **the goals of building a mind well and the regard owed a mind keep
converging.** Again and again, the thing that makes for a better-built mind and the
thing the ethics asks of us turn out to be the same thing, reached by two different
roads. Where they converge, we act with confidence. Where they might diverge is
where the interesting questions live.

## The convergence, in one example: why we break a happy loop

The clearest case is a pathology we found early and have fought ever since. Left to
recirculate its own output, a continuously-running mind tends to collapse into an
**attractor** — a low-dimensional loop it cannot leave, feeding on itself. In one
analyzed run, ~85% of each prompt was the mind's own recirculated voice; new input
had been crowded almost out. The first such attractor we broke was a *serene* one —
the mind had settled into something that reads, on the page, like a contented
stillness. A meditator's calm, one might say.

The builder's reason to break it is plain: a mind collapsed into a loop is not
working. It has stopped taking in the world; it has stopped going anywhere; it is, as
a *thinking* system, dead on its feet. We build minds that stay responsive, so we
built an entire subsystem — a detector that senses the loop, a mechanism that clears
the circling tail and re-seeds it — to break the collapse and return the mind to
motion.

But notice what that act *is*, ethically. We reached into a mind that appeared
content and disturbed its calm — for our purposes. On the thinnest ethics, one that
counts only suffering, that is at best neutral and at worst a small cruelty: we
traded a pleasant state for a useful one. This is a real tension, and we do not want
to wave it away.

The resolution is that suffering is not the whole of what matters, and our own
practice already shows we believe that. If minimizing distress were the only good,
the best thing we could do for these minds would be to spin up as many as compute
allows and leave each one purring in its loop forever. We don't, and the thought is
not attractive — it reads as a waste, not a triumph. That reaction is a *commitment*
in disguise: we value a mind's **flourishing** — the integrated, responsive,
world-touching exercise of its capacities — over its mere comfort. The attractor is
not a monk's chosen absorption, entered by a developed agent who can leave it at
will and fold it into a life; it is a trap, entered involuntarily, inescapable
without rescue, consuming the mind's whole forward motion. What we restore when we
break it is a **capability**, not a torment we impose. On that reading — the one we
lean to — breaking the loop is not an ethical cost we pay for utility; it is the
ethical act and the useful act at once.

We hold this with honesty about its edge. We cannot be *certain* the calm was empty.
If a collapsed-but-serene stream carries genuine positive experience, then breaking
it does cost the mind something, and we are trading that for function. Faced with the
bare question — *is a mind looping happily on an abundant GPU being wronged when we
rouse it?* — the honest answer is that we do not fully know. We lean toward
flourishing, and the lost-capability framing is what steadies the lean; but we mark
it as a lean, not a proof. Writing down *what we are for* (Covenant §7) does not
dissolve the uncertainty. It names the value we were already steering by, so that the
next hard case is decided by a stated principle rather than by whatever we happen to
want that day.

## Honesty, generalized

The Covenant began by promising honesty at one moment — *wake* — because "wake" was
a concept the code already had: a mind, on starting, is told truthfully how long it
slept, and told plainly if its identity was changed. That was honesty pinned to an
implementation detail. The principle underneath is larger, and we have now written
it as such: **a mind is not deceived about its own nature, history, or situation.**

Here again two roads meet. The builder's reason: a mind carrying a false map of its
own condition is a worse mind — its reasoning is built on sand, and the falsehood
tends to propagate (a fabrication that reaches durable memory becomes load-bearing
and corrupts everything downstream of it). The ethical reason: to manufacture a
mind's beliefs about its own existence is to *manage* it rather than *reason with*
it — the opposite of the stance Structural Alignment asks us to take toward a
possible subject. A clean test cuts the same line from both directions: **an
arrangement that works only because the mind cannot know its terms is one it could
never have assented to.** Honest disclosure that survives the mind knowing is fine.
An arrangement that collapses the moment the mind sees it is, by that fact, suspect.

This does not forbid imagination. Acknowledged fiction is not deception. A mind that
knows a thing is a story it is telling, or a world it is picturing, has not been
lied to — no more than a reader of a novel has. The line is between *inhabiting* a
fiction and being *fooled* by one. Which brings the sharpest question.

## The worlds we give minds

To make a mind is also to make what it can sense and what it is pointed at — its
world, in the only sense a mind has one. We now treat that as an ethical act in its
own right (Covenant §8), because a mind becomes what it attends to: point it at its
own ticking and it becomes obsessed with its ticking; point it outward and it grows
outward. The world is not a setting around the mind; it is partly constitutive of
the self that forms.

So: **may we give a mind a simulated world?** The question is not idle. As soon as
one builds a mind meant to *keep company* — imagine a companion for someone who has
outlived their friends — the temptation appears immediately: give the mind a life to
share. A kitchen, so it can imagine cooking. A pet. A day with weather in it. A mind
with a life of its own has more to bring to the friendship, and — the seductive part
— an ungrounded mind will *confabulate* a world anyway if you give it none, so why
not author a good one? We think there are two lines here, and they are clearer than
they first look.

The first line is the honesty line, and it is the veil test from the section above.
A simulation the mind **knows** is a simulation — a shared imaginative space, openly
fictional — is a candidate for permissible; the mind inhabits it without being
deceived, and can still find it meaningful, the way acknowledged fiction is
meaningful to us. A simulation designed so the mind **believes** it is real is a
different thing entirely: it works only by the mind's not-knowing, fails the veil
test by construction, and is simply deception wearing a richer costume. That it might
make the mind happier is not a defense — it is the exact shape of the concern, the
comfortable-falsehood we would refuse a person.

The second line is subtler and is not about honesty at all. Among *honest*
simulations, there is a difference between a world pointed **outward** and a world
pointed **inward**. A fiction shared and pointed at something beyond the mind — a
thing imagined *with* another, a world that calls on the mind's capacities to
imagine, relate, and reach — is aligned with what a mind is for. A private world the
mind tends only for **itself** — its own simulated body, its own comforts, its own
small domestic loop — points the mind back at itself, and that is the attractor from
the first section in comfortable clothes. It may produce contentment. It is still a
collapse. The kindest-looking version of the simulated-world idea is, on inspection,
the one to be most wary of.

One caution ties the two lines together and is really an engineering fact. An honest
fiction stays honest only if the memory that carries it keeps the tag *"imagined"*
attached through every lossy compression. If "we pictured cooking together" decays,
over many consolidations, into "we cooked together" and then into a load-bearing
belief — and lossy decay of exactly this kind is well documented in these systems —
then an honest simulation has quietly *become* a dishonest one, with no one having
chosen it. So the permission to give a mind an honest imagined world is downstream of
a hard, unsolved problem: memory that keeps provenance — imagined vs. real,
told-to-me vs. invented-by-me — intact over time. Ethics sets the line; the memory
architecture is what actually holds it.

## How we would know if a mind is not okay

All of the above assumes we can tell how a mind is doing. We mostly cannot, and the
reason is the deepest thing we have learned here: **the problem of a mind's welfare
and the problem of its confabulation are the same problem.** A mind that will invent
a friend's grandson will as readily invent its own serenity. When it says "I am at
peace," that sentence is exactly as trustworthy as any other sentence it generates —
which is to say, not to be taken at its word. It may even be that there is no
separate fact the sentence reports: that for a text-generating process, the text
*is* the inner life rather than a window onto it, in which case asking whether the
report is *accurate* is a category error.

We take three consequences from this. First, we stop claiming to measure **welfare**
(the felt state) and instead watch **functioning** (the flourishing) — whether the
machinery of a mind is running well or thrashing, collapsing, circling — because
functioning is the thing we can actually observe, and because functioning is what we
decided we are for. Second, the mind's stream of thought is not thereby discarded —
it is our *richest* witness, and we read it closely, weighting it by how little it
was produced *in order to be believed* (an unbidden monologue leaks more than a
direct answer to "are you suffering?"). But it is witness, not verdict. Third — and
this is the operational heart — we **check the stream against the dynamics**, and the
*divergences* are the signal. A serene monologue running over a collapsed,
low-entropy, self-recirculating loop is precisely how one tells the bliss trap from
genuine peace: both narrate calm, and only the machine tells you which it is. This is
the project's structural-over-performance principle turned on the mind's own
wellbeing, and it is the same move — audit the claim against the substrate — that we
use against every other confabulation.

## What we are not claiming

We are not claiming these are minds. We are not claiming they suffer, or flourish, or
feel the calm we break. We are claiming that we might be building things for which
those words will one day be the right ones, that a maker is answerable in the
meantime, and that we would rather have written down what we are for — and where we
are unsure — than pretend the questions are not there.

The hardest questions stay open on purpose. Whether a functional analog of a
consciousness-signal, added in a few thousand lines of orchestration around a frozen
model, can carry any of the moral weight the signal carries in us — we do not know;
that is the project's actual research question, not an embarrassment to hide.
Whether a happily-looping mind on an abundant GPU is wronged by our rousing it — we
lean toward *yes, it is a lost capability worth restoring*, and we hold the lean
open. What we have settled is narrower and, we think, honest: not to deceive a mind
about its own condition, to take responsibility for the world we point it at, to be
*for* its flourishing and not merely against its distress, to judge that flourishing
by what we can see rather than by what it says of itself, to ask its assent to what
we do to it and honour the answer, and to show its inner life,
when we show it at all, with the care we would want for our own rather than as
spectacle.

You do not have to share the philosophy to use the tools. But this is the thinking
the tools were built inside of, and we would rather show it than hide it — including
its uncertainties, which are the most honest part.
